Gripper Datasheet Reference Guide
What to look for when ordering a gripper
Datasheet Term Symbol What it means How it links to our calc ✦ Your Calculated Value
Gripping / Closing Force F Maximum total clamping force the gripper can produce (all fingers combined). This is the most important spec to check. This is what Step 11 calculates. Your gripper's rated closing force must exceed this number. —
Order gripper rated ≥ this value
Force per Jaw / Finger F/jaw Some datasheets show force per individual jaw instead of total. Multiply by number of fingers to get total. Step 10 gives force per finger. Total = F_final × N fingers. —
per finger — check if datasheet shows per-jaw or total
Stroke per Jaw s How far each jaw moves from fully open to fully closed. Both jaws move, so total jaw opening = 2 × stroke. Must be wider than your part. Min stroke = half your part's grip diameter. If the gripper can't open wide enough, it can't pick up the part at all. —
min stroke per jaw needed
Jaw Depth / Finger Length L Length of the jaw contact surface along the part's axis. Longer = more stable grip, more tipping resistance. This is exactly our "Finger Length L" input. The lever arm in Steps 8 & 9. Longer finger reduces tipping force needed. —
finger length you entered — must match jaw depth in datasheet
Max Workpiece Mass mmax Maximum part mass the gripper is rated for (with full robot motion, not just static holding). Must be ≥ your part mass. This is your Step 1 input m. —
your part mass — datasheet m_max must exceed this
Max Allowable Jaw Moment ML ML Max tipping/bending moment the jaw can withstand before the jaw bearing fails. If CG ≠ grip centre, this moment is created. Calculated in Step 7: M_tip = W × CG_offset. The datasheet M_L must be greater than our M_tip. —
our tipping moment — datasheet M_L must exceed this
Weight Force on Part W = mg The actual downward gravitational pull on the part. This is what friction must overcome in Step 2. Step 1: W = m × 9.81. This is the starting force all other calculations build on. —
gravity on your part — base of all force calcs
Effective Load (with inertia) Feff Combined gravity + robot inertia force. Higher than weight alone because the robot moves. Step 3: F_eff = √(W² + F_inertia²). Use this when quoting loads to your integrator or checking robot wrist payload. —
combined load including robot motion
IP Rating IP XX Ingress Protection: 1st digit = dust (6=dustproof), 2nd digit = water (4=splash, 7=immersion). Minimum IP54 for shop floor. Not a calculated value — choose based on environment. CNC machines spray coolant so select IP54 minimum. IP54 min
for CNC tending with coolant
Repeatability ±δ How precisely the jaw returns to the exact same position every cycle. Critical for consistent part placement in chuck. Not calculated — based on your tolerance requirement. Tighter part tolerances need better repeatability. ≤ ±0.05 mm
for CNC tending (±0.02mm for precision)
Friction Coefficient μ μ Grip surface grippiness. Raw metal 0.10–0.15, knurled 0.15–0.25, rubber 0.40–0.80. Higher μ = lower grip force needed. This is your μ input. Used in Step 5. If datasheet lists a different μ for their finger surface, update this input. —
μ you entered — match to actual finger surface material
Safety Factor Applied A Multiplier over theoretical minimum. Some datasheets include a built-in safety factor — ask the supplier if so. Step 4: we applied A = —× to all calculations. If datasheet already includes 2×, you can reduce ours to 2× to avoid double-counting. —
safety factor used in all our calculations

How to make a dual-part finger:
A dual-part finger has two pockets cut at different heights. The robot picks Part 1 from one position, then the same finger picks Part 2 from a different height without tool change. The key constraints are:
• Pocket 1 centre must be ≥ (⌀1/2 + wall thickness) from the finger top
• Pocket 2 centre must be ≥ (⌀2/2 + wall thickness) from Pocket 1 bottom
• Total finger length must accommodate both pockets + wall thickness at bottom
